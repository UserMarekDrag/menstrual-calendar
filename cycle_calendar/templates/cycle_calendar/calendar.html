The menstrual cycle is a natural process that occurs in women of reproductive age, and it is divided into four phases: the menstrual phase, follicular phase, ovulation phase, and luteal phase.

First phase

Menstrual phase: The menstrual phase is the first phase of the menstrual cycle, and it lasts from 3 to 6 days. During this phase, the uterus sheds its lining, and this results in the bleeding that is commonly known as a period.

Second phase

Follicular phase: The follicular phase is the second phase of the menstrual cycle and lasts from 7 to 21 days. During this phase, follicles in the ovary begin to mature and prepare for ovulation. The follicles produce estrogen, which helps to thicken the lining of the uterus, preparing it for the possible implantation of a fertilized egg.

Third phase

Ovulation phase: The ovulation phase is the third phase of the menstrual cycle and lasts for around 24 hours. During this phase, a mature follicle in the ovary ruptures, releasing an egg. The egg then travels down the fallopian tube, where it may be fertilized by sperm. This is the most fertile phase of the menstrual cycle.

Fourth phase

Luteal phase: The luteal phase is the final phase of the menstrual cycle and lasts for around 14 days. During this phase, the ruptured follicle in the ovary forms a structure called the corpus luteum. The corpus luteum produces progesterone, which helps to thicken the lining of the uterus, preparing it for the possible implantation of a fertilized egg. If the egg is not fertilized, the corpus luteum breaks down, and the menstrual cycle begins again.

When are my fertile days?

  • Body temperature increase. Regular temperature measurements allow you to determine when you are actually approaching ovulation. During ovulation, the body temperature reaches around 37 degrees Celsius.
  • Cervical mucus. During ovulation, its amount increases, and its consistency changes from thin to thick, resembling egg white.
  • Abdominal pain. During ovulation, many women experience strong abdominal or pelvic pain (in the area of the ovaries).
  • Changes in the cervix. During fertile days, the cervix develops and is tilted forward.

What factors can affect the timing of fertile days?

Remember that an ovulation calculator and observation of your own body do not give a complete guarantee of accurate calculation of fertile days. There are several factors that can affect the timing of ovulation and fertile days, including:

  • high stress levels,
  • illness in the first phase of the cycle and taking antibiotics,
  • high physical exertion,
  • diet and weight loss,
  • changes in climate,
  • lack of sleep,
  • overall body fatigue.

How to use a menstrual cycle calculator?

An online menstrual cycle calculator is a convenient solution for women who want to monitor their menstrual cycles. Regularly filling in data, such as entering the start dates of each period, allows the calculator to make more accurate predictions. By entering the dates for the entire year, you create a menstrual cycle calendar for the whole year.

Keep in mind that a menstrual cycle calculator should not be used as a form of contraception. Why? Even the most regular menstrual cycle can be disrupted by factors independent of the woman, such as illness, stress, travel, or simply exhaustion.

How does a menstrual cycle calculator work?

Based on the data you enter, you will receive predictions about your next period and upcoming fertile days.

How to calculate your period?

It's a good idea to prepare for your period. To calculate when it will start, you need to take into account the average length of your menstrual cycle, as well as the start date and length of your last period.

The period usually lasts from 4 to 7 days, with an average length of 4-5 days. During this time, under the influence of changes in the concentration of sex hormones, the endometrium (lining) of the uterus is shed. During this time, and also before your period, you may experience characteristic symptoms such as abdominal pain, mood and appetite swings, weight gain, and skin problems.

To calculate when your period will end, you need to estimate how many days it lasted over the last six months and take the average from those calculations. For example, if your period lasted 5, 6, 5, 7, 7, and 5 days over the last six months, the average length would be just under 6 days.
